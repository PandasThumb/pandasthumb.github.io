<figcaption><i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwigia_peruviana">Sphodros atlanticus</a></i> &ndash; purseweb spider.  Mr. Denelsbeck writes, "The business end of a male purseweb spider. While noticeable from above and seeming to take up far more of the spider's body mass than should be necessary, the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chelicerae">chelicerae</a> are most impressive from underneath, with very large fangs and serrated edges for gripping prey. Despite that, these are not large spiders &ndash; my specimen here is only 10-12&nbsp;mm in body length. The females are almost never seen, generally remaining inside the lair that provides their name, usually looking like a small twig against a treetrunk (I've searched, but found nothing of the sort so far). Canon 7D, Canon 18-135 STM at 64&nbsp;mm and 36&nbsp;mm extension tube, ISO 200, F/13, 1/200&nbsp;s with custom softbox flash unit."
